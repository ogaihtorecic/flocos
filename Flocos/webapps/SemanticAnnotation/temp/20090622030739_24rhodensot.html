Vince Young Has Gone From Stardom to the Shadows

By WILLIAM C. RHODEN
Published: November 23, 2008

As you approach the Tennessee Titans’ home field — L P Field — from the East, you are greeted by two larger-than-life images. The first belongs to Bruce Matthews, the first Titan to be inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame. As you round the corner, you’re met by the next prominent image: quarterback Vince Young.

Young has plummeted from being the onetime face of the franchise to warming the bench. Indeed, but for that larger-than-life image, Young would be the forgotten Titan.

The opening paragraph of Young’s 2008 bio says: “In two seasons since being selected with the third overall pick in the 2006 N.F.L. Draft, quarterback Vince Young has helped reinvigorate the Tennessee Titans and lead them back to playoff contention.”

On Sunday, Young watched Brett Favre outplay Kerry Collins as the Jets convincingly snapped Tennessee’s unbeaten streak, 34-13. Among other things, the loss may be a signal to Tennessee that as the water gets deeper, the Titans may need less of a manager at quarterback and more of a playmaker.

In an ideal world, this is where Young could step in. The pressure of rising too high too fast may not allow him.

Young became a starter in his fourth N.F.L. game in 2006 and set a number of rookie passing records for the Titans. He was the first rookie quarterback since 1966 to rush for 500 yards and was named Associated Press rookie of the year.

Now Young is the forgotten star.

How could one player fall so far, so fast?

Young spent much of the game Sunday as a Titans cheerleader.

At the beginning, he was at the end of the receiving line for opening introductions as the Titans’ defense was introduced. Young then trotted to the south end of the end zone, kneeled with four other players and said a prayer.

As Collins and Chris Simms, the Titans’ third quarterback, warmed up, Young sat on the bench. Whenever the Titans had the ball, Young stood at the far end of the field, behind the Jets’ defense, arms folded, chatting with Simms.

So, this was Vince Young’s day. Wearing a heavy jacket, pumping up his team, waiting, watching. Hopefully learning. Stars have certainly fallen before, but I can’t remember a star falling so steeply so suddenly.

Beyond the implications of a huge game between the undefeated Titans and the surging Jets on Sunday, I was intrigued by Young.

Before Sunday’s game, I spoke with Kevin Dyson, a Titans wide receiver from 1998 to 2002. Dyson felt Young’s primary issue was maturity, learning how to deal with adversity after seasons of uninterrupted success. He called Young a gamer and predicted that Young would make his way back, perhaps as early as next season.

Like many, I became intrigued with Young after his magical performance against Matt Leinart and U.S.C. in the 2006 B.C.S. title game. Young led Texas to an upset victory with two rushing touchdowns in the fourth quarter.

Now Young and Leinart are relegated to backup roles. Young behind the 35-year-old Collins, and Leinart behind 37-year-old Kurt Warner.

Things unraveled for Young in this season’s opener, when he was booed after throwing his second interception in a 17-10 victory over Jacksonville. When it appeared that Young didn’t want to return to the game, Coach Jeff Fisher pulled his headset off and talked to Young, who eventually joined the rest of the offense. Four plays later, he sprained a ligament in his left knee, and Collins has been the quarterback ever since.

But there was more drama than the booing. Young missed an M.R.I. exam the next day and didn’t report to Titans headquarters. The team sent a psychologist and another team official to Young’s home to talk with him. Young left. He said he left the house because he needed space to think. “Let the cloud go away for a minute, and that’s what I did,” he said. “I left.”

Fisher called the Nashville police, who searched for Young for more than four hours. According to The City Paper of Nashville, which obtained a copy of the police report through a public records request, the Titans called the police Monday because Young’s therapist told Fisher that Young had mentioned suicide several times before driving away from his home with a gun.

A supplemental report filed Tuesday by the Nashville police showed that Fisher was worried about Young after the call from the therapist. “I asked him, ‘What made her worry about him?’ ” Lt. Andrea Swisher wrote, referring to the therapist. “He stated, ‘His mood, his emotions, he wants to quit, and he mentioned suicide several times.’ He went on to state that he left the house with a gun.”

Young’s benching is the end, for now at least, of new-age quarterbacks on the brink of redefining of the quarterback position. With Michael Vick behind bars, Young on the bench and Donovan McNabb morphed more or less into a pocket passer, the league’s quarterback pool has reverted to the statue-like signal-callers who hang tough in the pocket, protected by rules designed to keeping defensive linemen and linebackers at bay.

Young finished Sunday’s game standing by himself standing on the Titans’ sideline watching the Jets thrash his previously unbeaten Titans.

They may need him yet.
